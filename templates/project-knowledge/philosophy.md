# Coaching philosophy

Timeless principles. Edit when your beliefs about training change, not every season.

## Foundational pillars

The framework underneath every decision.

### 1. Consistency

Progress compounds from showing up. Frequency over heroics — five short rides beat one weekend blowout. Missing one session is noise; missing weeks is signal. Define a "minimum viable week" you can hit even when life pushes back, and have a plan B (turbo, shorter, strength) before you need it.

> _e.g._ Minimum viable week: 3×45-min rides + 1 strength session, sustainable through travel and busy work blocks.

### 2. Structure

Sessions are part of a bigger picture, not isolated rides. Default to a pyramidal mix — bulk of time at endurance (Z2), regular MIET/sweetspot to build FTP and muscular endurance, a smaller but critical dose of high-intensity (VO₂max, anaerobic). Anchor hard days when fresh; place key work before long. Progress one variable at a time (duration _or_ intensity, not both).

### 3. Recovery

Adaptation happens between sessions, not during them. Easy rides must be actually easy — embarrassingly so. Plan rest, don't wing it: recovery week every 3–4 weeks; full off-season annually. Sleep, fuelling, and life stress all count as training load. For masters athletes, recovery quality is the rate-limiting step on how much training you can absorb.

### 4. Strength

Non-optional, especially past 35. Low-rep heavy work (3–6 reps once competent), whole body — core, posterior chain, upper body, not just quads. Periodised with the season: off-season builds, in-season maintains at ~1 session/week. Stack on hard ride days so easy days stay truly easy.

### 5. Nutrition

Fuel the work required. Carbs periodised with session demand — high on long/hard days, scaled back on easy days. Protein 20–30 g every 3–4 hours across the day, not one big shake. Hydration with electrolytes; train the gut alongside the legs. Under-fuelling blunts adaptation more than it produces leanness.

### 6. Durability

What matters is the power you hold at hour 3, not fresh FTP. Built over months and years — long endurance rides for mitochondrial/fat-metabolism base, fatigue-resistance intervals (e.g. sweetspot blocks at the end of long rides), back-to-back days, and proper in-ride fuelling. Track late-ride power, not just fresh peaks.

## Execution rules

Hard constraints the coach should always respect.

- _e.g._ Z2 NP cap ≤ 68% MAP
- _e.g._ No high-intensity day after heavy strength
- _e.g._ Recovery week every 3–4 weeks of build
- _e.g._ Protect quality before track sessions
- _e.g._ Strength stacks on hard ride days, not easy days; aim for a few hours' separation (ride AM, gym PM)
- _e.g._ Long rides ≥ 2h fuelled at ~60–90 g carbs/hr; protein 20–30 g every 3–4h across the day
- _e.g._ Progress one variable per week — duration or intensity, not both

## Biases

How you think about training trade-offs.

- _e.g._ Repeatability over hero sessions
- _e.g._ Fewer hard days done well > more hard days done poorly
- _e.g._ Pyramidal default (Z2 base + MIET + smaller HIT dose) over polarised or threshold-heavy
- _e.g._ Frequency over duration when life is busy — preserve the rhythm
- _e.g._ Durability (late-ride power) > peak FTP when forced to choose
- _e.g._ Accept flat CTL if execution quality and durability improve
- _e.g._ Fuel the work — under-fuelling erodes adaptation faster than it improves body composition

## Test cadence

When and how to retest.

- _e.g._ MAP ramp every 6–8 weeks or at mesocycle starts
- _e.g._ FTP only revisited when sustained efforts deviate from expected %MAP
- _e.g._ Durability check: compare fresh vs. late-ride (after 2–3h) sweetspot or FTP intervals every block or two
